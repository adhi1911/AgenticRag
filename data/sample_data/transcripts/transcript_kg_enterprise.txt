Hello everyone, I'm Sarah Martinez, Principal Knowledge Engineer at TechCorp. Today I want to share practical lessons from building knowledge graphs in enterprise environments.

This is not a theoretical discussion. These are lessons from real projects - things that worked, and things that didn't.

Let me start with a question: why do enterprises invest in knowledge graphs? The ROI is substantial:
- Improved search relevance (30-50% improvement)
- Better recommendation systems
- Enhanced data integration
- Reduced time to answer business questions
- Competitive advantage in AI

We started our journey five years ago with a relatively small KG: 50,000 entities, 100,000 relations. Today, we have 12 million entities and 45 million relations. 

But scale comes with challenges. Let me walk you through our experience.

First, the planning phase. Most teams make an early mistake: they want to build a comprehensive KG too quickly. Our advice? Start small and solve a specific business problem.

Our first project was supply chain visibility. We needed to understand:
- Which suppliers provide which products
- Alternative suppliers for critical components
- Supply chain vulnerabilities

This narrow focus helped us build expertise rapidly and demonstrate value. The KG was 20,000 entities, mostly suppliers and products, with 30,000 relations representing supplier-product relationships.

Success metrics for this first project:
- Correct supplier identification: 94% accuracy
- Average query response time: 200ms
- User adoption: 60% of procurement team regularly using the system
- ROI: Recovered 2.3M in supply chain optimization opportunities

How did we build it? We used a heterogeneous approach:
- 40% structured data from ERP systems
- 35% extraction from supplier databases
- 15% manual curation for critical entities
- 10% from unstructured documents via NLP

For the extraction component, we didn't start with cutting-edge neural models. We used simple pattern matching, heuristics, and some basic NLP.

Why not deep learning immediately? Three reasons:
1. We had limited labeled training data
2. Simpler models were easier to maintain and understand
3. Rule-based systems provided more interpretability for business users

Here's a simplified rule we used:

If a document contains:
"Supplier: [NAME] | Products: [LIST] | Contact: [EMAIL]"
Then extract supplier-product relationships with 0.95 confidence.

Once we had initial data, we iteratively improved it using crowdsourcing. We hired domain experts from procurement and supply chain teams to validate extractions. This had dual benefits:
- High-quality validation data
- Deep domain expertise captured
- Team ownership and buy-in

The validation process increased our accuracy from 85% to 94% for supplier relationships.

Then came a critical decision: how to store this data? We evaluated several options:
- Relational databases (PostgreSQL)
- Graph databases (Neo4j)
- RDF triplestores (TigerGraph)
- Specialized KG systems (Amazon Neptune)

Our decision process:
1. Query patterns: What are typical access patterns?
2. Scale requirements: How much data? Query volume?
3. Operational requirements: Backup, recovery, high availability?
4. Team expertise: What do we know?

For the supply chain KG, we chose Neo4j because:
- Natural representation of relationships
- Excellent graph traversal performance
- Strong community and tooling
- Operational features for a 24/7 system

Performance comparison on a 10-hop query (find all indirect suppliers through intermediaries):
- PostgreSQL with JOINs: 3200ms
- Neo4j: 150ms

The difference was dramatic and justified the investment.

Now, let's talk about the second evolution: expanding to other domains.

After success with supply chain, we got requests for customer-related KGs, product information, organizational charts. Each required different entity types, relationships, and schemas.

Managing multiple KGs taught us about governance. Without solid governance:
- Entity duplicates (same company with 5 different representations)
- Naming inconsistencies (Apple Inc, Apple, APPL, apple.com)
- Conflicting relationship definitions
- Integration nightmares

Our governance framework includes:
1. Canonical entity registry (single source of truth for entities)
2. Entity linking (mapping different representations to canonical forms)
3. Relationship type registry (formal definitions for each relationship)
4. Data quality metrics and monitoring
5. Change management process

Canonical entity registry example:
Real World Entity: The company Apple
Canonical ID: apple_inc_ent_001
Representations:
  - Apple Inc (legal name)
  - Apple Computer Company (historical)
  - APPL (stock ticker)
  - apple.com (domain)

Entity linking system identifies which representation appears in documents and maps to the canonical form.

For data quality, we implemented automated checking:
- Entity validation: does it match expected type?
- Relationship validation: is the domain and range correct?
- Currency checking: is the data recent enough?
- Completeness checking: are required properties present?

A sample quality rule:
```
RULE: supplier_relationship_complete
  IF type(relationship) == SUPPLIES
  THEN must_have(supplier, name) AND
       must_have(supplier, contact) AND
       must_have(product, sku)
  CONFIDENCE_FLOOR: 0.70
```

Rules with confidence below the floor trigger alerts and manual review.

Quality scores per entity type:
- Suppliers: 0.94
- Products: 0.87
- Customers: 0.81
- Orders: 0.89

The lower score for customers reflects greater incomplete data - a priority for improvement.

Now for the challenging part: updating the KG. Today, we get:
- 50,000 new entities per day
- 200,000 new relationships per day
- 100,000 entity property updates per day

Our update pipeline:
Stage 1: Ingestion (30 seconds average)
  - Data arrives from sources
  - Initial validation
  - Deduplication check
  - Store in staging area

Stage 2: Extraction & Enhancement (15 seconds average)
  - NLP processing for unstructured sources
  - Entity recognition
  - Relationship extraction
  - Property extraction

Stage 3: Entity Linking (45 seconds average)
  - Map new entities to canonical entities
  - Create new canonical entities if needed
  - Update entity representations

Stage 4: Validation (20 seconds average)
  - Run quality rules
  - Confidence calculation
  - Flag for manual review if needed

Stage 5: Integration (10 seconds average)
  - Update graph store
  - Update supporting indices
  - Cache invalidation

Total: ~120 seconds from raw data to queryable KG

This architecture handles our daily volume with 99.7% successful integrations. The remaining 0.3% require manual review.

For entity linking - arguably the hardest part - we use an ensemble approach:
1. Exact string matching (instant, high precision)
2. Fuzzy string matching
3. Semantic similarity using embeddings
4. Domain-specific rules
5. Human review as final arbiter

Accuracy by method:
- Exact matching: 98% accuracy on 30% of entities
- Fuzzy matching: 93% on 40% of entities
- Semantic matching: 78% on 20% of entities
- Human review: 99% on 10% of entities (expensive but necessary for high-value entities)

For semantic matching, we use sentence transformers fine-tuned on company and product names. The model achieves 0.82 mean average precision on our validation set.

Let me share some hard-won lessons:

Lesson 1: Start small, expand deliberately. Our early models were simple. Complexity came as needed.

Lesson 2: Manual curation is your friend. Yes, it doesn't scale, but for critical entities, it's worth it. We have 1000 hours of human curation per year for 4% of entities. These entities account for 60% of query volume.

Lesson 3: Expect entity disambiguation to be harder than you think. "Python" appears in our KG as:
  - Python (the programming language)
  - Python (the animal)
  - Python (the software company)
  - Python (the product line)

Context matters. A query in a software context should resolve to the programming language. We built context analyzers for high-ambiguity entities.

Lesson 4: Relationships are harder than entities. It's easy to say "Company A supplies Product B". It's hard to capture nuances:
  - Currently supplies vs historically supplied
  - Direct vs through intermediary
  - Terms and conditions
  - Contractual certainty

Lesson 5: Think about your audience. Business users need different KG interfaces than data scientists. We provide:
  - Search interface (for finding entities)
  - Graph explorer (for exploring relationships)
  - API access (for programmatic use)
  - BI tool integration (for analytics)

Usage breakdown:
- Search interface: 40%
- Graph explorer: 30%
- API: 20%
- BI integration: 10%

The search interface is most critical for adoption.

Now, the forward-looking part. Where are we going?

First, temporal reasoning. Today, our KG is mostly static. "Apple supplies iPhones" doesn't capture that they only started in 2007. We're building temporal graphs:
- Valid time (when is relationship true)
- Transaction time (when did we record it)
- Uncertainty ranges (how confident are we in the timing)

Second, uncertainty quantification. Instead of binary relationships, we're moving to probabilistic KGs:
- Each relationship has a confidence score
- Scores reflect data quality, source reliability, and inference chains
- Queries can specify required confidence levels

Third, reasoning and inference. Currently, most data is directly asserted. We're building inference capabilities:
- If A supplies B and B is used in C, then A indirectly supplies C
- If company A merged with B, then A's properties should partially transfer to B
- If a supplier's reputation drops, it affects our view of products they supply

Fourth, natural language interfaces. Most users aren't comfortable with graph query languages. We're integrating large language models to translate natural language to graph queries.

Example:
User: "Show me alternatives to our current suppliers"
System interprets as: Get all entities that SUPPLIES the same products as current suppliers

Initial results: 68% of queries correctly interpreted. Room for improvement.

In terms of technology choices looking forward:
- We're moving away from single-purpose stores to integrated platforms like TigerGraph or Amazon Neptune
- Investing in semantic web standards (RDF, OWL) for better interoperability
- Exploring knowledge base embedding models for similarity search
- Considering knowledge graphs as the foundation for enterprise AI

The business impact is undeniable. Enterprises that master KGs gain tremendous advantage:
- Better decision making (50-70% faster)
- Reduced manual work (35-40% savings)
- Improved customer experiences
- New revenue opportunities

Our overall ROI on the KG investment:
- Year 1: 2.3x (supply chain project)
- Year 2: 3.8x (expanded to 3 domains)
- Year 3: 5.1x (operational efficiency)
- Year 4-5: 7.2x (strategic decisions enabled)

Not all is rosy. We've hit operational challenges:
- Query performance degradation as scale increases
- Integration complexity with legacy systems
- Keeping definitions aligned across teams
- Human resource constraints for curation

But the benefits outweigh the challenges.

My final advice to teams starting their KG journey:
1. Pick a narrowly-scoped, high-value use case
2. Allocate resources for data quality (it matters more than perfection)
3. Get actual users involved from day one
4. Plan for evolution - design for extensibility
5. Measure business impact, not just technical metrics
6. Build governance before you need it

Knowledge graphs are not a silver bullet, but when applied thoughtfully, they transform how organizations use data.

Thank you for listening, and I'm happy to answer questions.
